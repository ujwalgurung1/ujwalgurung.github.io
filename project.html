Housing is the principal source of household wealth in most countries around the world. Households in the top of the wealth distribution hold more diversified portfolios, less wealthy households own mostly housing and least wealthy households own virtually nothing with some even is net debt.

We have estalished link between homeownership rates and wealth inequality. Promoting homeownership has long been a policy objective. This has often been justified on grounds that homeownership can help households accumulate wealth and save for retirement, as well as contribute to well-being and social cohesion. However, governemnt policies tend to have unwanted effects. One such factor includes social mobility. Social mobility refers to the movement of individuals either up or down the socioeconomic ladder relative to their current standing, such as a low-income family moving up to become a part of the middle class. Countries with high levels of social mobility exhibit lower levels of income inequality and provide more equally shared opportunities for its citizens. It can be argued that a high ownership environment in the UK has priced out many others who seek to move into areas of high economic opportunities such as London and other cities. this phenomenon is also know was geographical immoblity.
